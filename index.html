The Foundational Claim

Christianity is best understood as originating not in abstract belief or later theological reflection, but in a public claim about a historical event: that Jesus of Nazareth, executed by crucifixion, was raised from the dead and appeared to witnesses. Using standard historical criteria and without presupposing Christian doctrine, the earliest sources indicate that the Christian movement arose from the open testimony of named individuals and groups who claimed to have encountered the risen Jesus, and who proclaimed this claim from the beginning as the foundation of the movement.

At this stage, historical inquiry is limited to identifying what claims were made, by whom, when, and with what degree of sincerity and public attestation; it does not adjudicate ultimate causation or metaphysical plausibility. Accordingly, explanations are not excluded at this level solely on the basis of being classified as supernatural or miraculous, since such exclusions rest on prior philosophical commitments rather than historical criteria, and competing explanations for the origin of the resurrection proclamation must be evaluated by the same historical standards without privileging naturalistic accounts by default.

The theological question—what the resurrection signifies about the identity of Jesus—arises only after this historical description is complete, and it is at this interpretive stage, not at the level of historical acknowledgment, that belief and confession properly enter.
